Theory

Genetic Algorithm

The genetic algorithm is a process inspired by natural selection. It operates with successive generations. During one generation, we have a population composed of individuals, each of which has genes. Each individual will be evaluated during its life and receive a fitness score. At the end of the generation, individuals reproduce. This is where selection can occur: the higher an individual's score, the more likely it is to reproduce. Random mutations are then applied to the genomes of individuals, and the next generation begins.

Individuals

An individual corresponds to a car and its driver, which is itself a neural network. Each of its connections represents a gene. For more information, refer to the part of the guide on Neural Networks.

Population

The initial population is composed of randomly generated individuals. Subsequent populations are a product of the reproduction of the previous population.

Fitness

Individuals earn a point each time they cross a new reward line. For more information, refer to the part of the guide on Cars and Tracks.

Reproduction

The reproduction process works in two stages:

- Selection:
We want to find two parents by favoring the fittest individuals. To avoid always selecting the same ones, we use the concept of Social Mixing. We take a subgroup of the population, chosen randomly, and extract the individual with the highest fitness from it. We repeat the operation in another subgroup to obtain the second parent. Note: the parameter is adjusted in the Evolution tab as a percentage, which corresponds to the size of the subgroups relative to the total population. Lowering this parameter adds diversity.

- Merging:
The reproduction step is inspired by nature in a very simplified version. For each gene, there is a fifty-fifty chance of inheriting it from parent 1. There is no organization into chromosomes, for example.

We repeat the process until we have the right number of individuals in the next population.

Mutation

We introduce mutations into the genes of each individual. The mutation rate corresponds to the probability for each gene to be replaced by a random value.

Parallel with Our World

Environment

The environment in the context of Neural Racing corresponds to the tracks. Individuals well adapted to one track will not always be suitable for others.

Try changing the tracks in the Experiments tab!

Phenotype

The phenotypes of individuals in the context of Neural Racing can be observed in the form of skills. On a track that starts with a left turn, for example, individuals will quickly acquire the "Ability to turn left" phenotype but will be unable to handle a right turn. Similarly, because time is limited, some individuals will score more points by accelerating on straight lines.

In the Evolution tab, insert an individual with a certain phenotype on a slightly different track to broaden the individuals' skills. Through iterations, you will have an individual adapted to many environments!

Genetic Diversity

We can easily explore this concept through the Mutation and Social Mixing parameters. Disable Include Champion, then try changing the parameters. For example, with a mutation rate of 100%, you will have purely random results. At 0%, you will have a convergence of individuals toward the same genome, which will be manifested by a peak on the histogram.
